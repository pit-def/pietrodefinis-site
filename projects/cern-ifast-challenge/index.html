Field story

Entering the experiment

Challenge-Based Innovation 2025 compressed ten days into a single question: how can particle accelerators improve human health? Joining The T.E.A.M. — Terrific Experts in Accelerating Medicine — meant embracing chaos, misunderstandings and the pressure of building something worthy of CERN.

Finding the concept

By asking “So what?” we pushed beyond theory and landed on an elegant intersection: an injectable bone cement activated under controlled X-ray exposure. X-Ray MAGIC blended bioactive materials with radioluminescent particles, making science fiction feel plausible.

Human chain reaction

The sprint rhythm was relentless, but trust turned intensity into flow. A supposed rest day in Annecy became the bonding moment that aligned us for the final stretch.

CERN’s Council Chamber

Presenting in the Council Chamber, where Nobel laureates debated physics, reframed my own journey. We didn’t win the competition, yet mentors called our concept the most feasible and high-impact. That recognition confirmed that innovation thrives on clarity, not theatrics.

After the sprint

I left CERN without a trophy but with perspective: the boy who built particle experiments in his garage had returned, now as a catalyst creating space for others to innovate. Curiosity remains the most powerful accelerator we have.

Process

  1. Framing. Reframed the challenge through interviews with mentors from CERN, HITRIplus and med-tech startups, identifying unmet needs in bone regeneration.
  2. Ideation. Co-created system maps and value propositions that balanced accelerator capabilities with clinical workflow realities.
  3. Validation. Prototyped interaction scripts, tested assumptions with domain experts and iterated on radiation safety scenarios.
  4. Storycraft. Crafted a multi-layer pitch that blended feasibility, impact and emotion for the final presentation at CERN.

Outcomes

  • X-Ray MAGIC recognised by mentors as the most feasible, high-impact proposal of the cohort.
  • Delivered a concept note and storyboard connecting material science, radiology and surgical needs.
  • Forged a network of collaborators extending beyond the program for future research opportunities.
  • Personal milestone achieved: entering CERN as an innovator, not a visitor.

Reflections

The sprint proved that innovation is a human chain reaction. Alignment and trust moved the project faster than any tool. Asking “So what?” at every turn ensured technology stayed grounded in patient value and societal impact.
