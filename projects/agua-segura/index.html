Objectives & motivation

Problem to solve

Rural communities relied on untreated river or well water, while municipal monitoring mandated by law rarely happened.

Main objective

Build a participatory water-quality system owned by the communities, using accessible scientific tools and a digital workflow.

Secondary objectives

  • Train local leaders to interpret field analyses and act on the results.
  • Ensure transparency with public dashboards and open data.
  • Promote environmental awareness and self-reliance across municipalities.

Personal drive

Transform a civil service placement into a real Project Catalyst experiment that merged chemistry, digital tools and human impact to make knowledge actionable.

Approach

Method

Citizen science + agile implementation

  1. Context analysis: mapped community needs and existing water governance.
  2. Co-design: defined monitoring protocols with partners to balance rigor and cultural practices.
  3. Prototype & test: piloted the workflow with one municipality before scaling.
  4. Digital backbone: transitioned from paper logs to Kobo forms, Make automations and live dashboards.
  5. Capacity building: authored an eight-module online course and facilitated experiential workshops.
  6. Communication: launched the Ojo al Agua public site and storytelling materials.

Key challenges

  • Bridging cultural and linguistic barriers through practice-first workshops.
  • Working with limited digital infrastructure and connectivity.
  • Designing a system that could scale while remaining low-maintenance.

Field story

From zero to a shared vision

When I arrived in the highlands of San Marcos with a six-month assignment, the partner organisation had never run an environmental monitoring project. Listening to leaders and families made the priority obvious: water safety. Instead of collecting samples myself, I proposed a citizen-science project that communities could own ‚Äî Agua Segura.

Designing the framework

The plan unfolded in three layers: empower leaders through training, activate a pilot municipality, and create a digital backbone to hold the system together. Workshops happened under tin roofs and beside rivers, where we translated chemistry into everyday practice and overcame language gaps with shared action.

Building the digital backbone

Paper tables sent over WhatsApp could not sustain the pace, so I rebuilt the workflow with Kobo Toolbox, Make automations and structured dashboards. In weeks we moved from fragmented spreadsheets to a real-time system that anyone with a phone could access, anchored by the Ojo al Agua public site.

Creating knowledge that stays

Technology without capacity fades. I wrote an eight-chapter online course in Spanish that explained each indicator and action. It became the onboarding tool for new community monitors and ensured the knowledge stayed once my assignment ended.

Outcomes and reflections

By the handover, three municipalities were active, fifteen community scientists were monitoring water, and the partners had a roadmap toward eleven municipalities. More than dashboards, the project shifted how science was perceived ‚Äî from distant expertise to shared responsibility. Agua Segura taught me that impact means building systems that survive you, blending scientific rigor with empathy and storytelling.
